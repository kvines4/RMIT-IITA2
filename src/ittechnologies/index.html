Machine Learning

Definition

Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence. The central to the discipline of machine learning is using existing training data in a software with algorithms to make predictions of future data. With enough training data (the more the better) computer can find patterns in data and start generating predictions with the highest probable outcome. When going through all that data computer “learns” and gains experience similarly how we humans learn.

How it works?

There are four types of machine learning: supervised learning, unsupervised learning, semi-supervised and reinforcement learning. Main difference between those types is the type of training data supplied.
Supervised learning algorithms give more control and reduce bias with applying what has been learned in the past to new data using labelled examples to predict future events. Predicted output values are based on known training datasets and can provide targets for new inputs after training. Furthermore, it gives the possibility to compare the outputs with correct training output and intended output to find errors and adjust the model as needed. (Selig, 2022)
With unsupervised machine learning the algorithm goes through the unlabelled data and tries to find patterns and trends. The algorithm segments data into clusters that have similar parameters basically creating a supervised learning dataset. Sample of unsupervised machine learning algorithm in action would be a email spam detection technology.
Semi supervised learning algorithm is kind of happy medium. It combines both labelled and unlabelled data. It can be very useful when labelling data is difficult decision for humans. It is commonly used with CT scans and MRI’s. A radiologist will label sample set of images with tumours and diseases and lets the algorithm analyse the dataset against the labelled images as a result improving its accuracy compared to unsupervised model. (Salian, 2022)
Reinforced learning model is sort of how dogs are trained, rewards are given when actions taken are successful towards solving the problem in hand. With reinforced learning model the algorithm does not have a sample data set, but goes through series of decisions, successful decisions will “reinforce”
The algorithm hence the name reinforced learning model. Reinforced learning model is most often used in robotics where programs run through millions of versions possible actions and choosing the ones most successful.
One of the main benefits of using machine learning is using computers processing powers to go through vast amounts of data and predicting outcomes based on that data. Because of machine learning requires processing of large amounts of data it used to be a tool reserved to large companies, but cloud computing has made it more affordable to even smaller businesses.

Autonomous Vehicles

How it works:

Autonomous vehicles (AV) have been developed to take either full or partial control of a vehicle. AV's can navigate through traffic while remaining aware of other vehicles and traffic conditions. These vehicles can be classified into a system of levels that describes the level of automation a vehicle has. This may vary from country to country, but a high-level vehicle can operate completely autonomous without a driver's assistance. However, a low-level AV may only be able to operate part/s of the vehicle and still depend on the driver to be in control. This can include systems like cruise control and lane assist; in both these examples, the driver will either need to keep the vehicle within a set of lane markings or maintain the vehicle's speed.

AV’s can do this since they use multiple technologies and sensors to detect what is in front of the vehicle while manoeuvring in a safe and controlled manner. Some technologies that are present in AV’s include:

  • Global Positioning Systems (GPS): This technology helps provide an estimated pinpoint of the vehicle through satellites.
  • Ultrasonic Sensors: These sensors help provide short distance data that the vehicle will need in its parking assistance systems.
  • Light Detection and ranging (LIDAR): LIDAR is a 360-degree sensor that uses light beams to help determine the distance between the obstacles and the sensor.
  • Radio Detection and ranging (RADAR): Similar to the LIDAR, this sensor uses radio waves to determine the distance between obstacles and the sensor itself.
  • Infrared Sensors: This technology helps the AV detect lane markings, pedestrians, bicycles, and other obstacles that the other sensors may not be able to detect in low light conditions.
  • Inertial Navigation Systems (INS): INS is used in conjunction with GPS to help determine the vehicle position, orientation, and velocity.
  • Dedicated short-range communication (DSRC): This is used in Vehicle to Vehicle (V2V) and Vehicle to Infrastructure (V2I) systems where the AV can send and receive important data such as conditions of a road, whether the road has congestion or a traffic accident and possible rerouting if deemed necessary by the system.
  • Cameras: With the help of complex algorithms, Cameras help the AV interpret images that are in front of the vehicle which can help the not only the AV determine what is in front of it, but also help the driver, such as parking assistance with a 360 view of the vehicle.

Future and Developments:

Optimists predict that by 2030, autonomous vehicles will be sufficiently reliable, affordable, and common to displace most human driving, providing huge savings and benefits. However, there are good reasons to be sceptical. People make the most optimistic predictions with financial interests in the industry based on experience with disruptive technologies such as digital cameras, smartphones and personal computers. They tend to ignore significant obstacles to autonomous vehicle development and exaggerate future benefits.
There is considerable uncertainty concerning autonomous vehicle development, demands, benefits, costs, and travel impacts. Operating a vehicle on public roads is complicated due to frequent interactions with often-unpredictable animals, people and vehicles. Considerable progress is needed before autonomous vehicles can operate reliably in mixed urban traffic, heavy rain and snow, unpaved and unmapped roads, and unreliable wireless service. In most countries, years of testing and regulatory approval are required before they are commercially available. The first commercially available autonomous vehicles are likely to be expensive, have limited performance, and introduce new risks. These constraints will limit sales. Many motorists will be reluctant to pay thousands of extra dollars for vehicles that are sometimes unable to reach a destination due to inclement weather or unmapped roads. Vehicles last longer, cost more, impose higher external costs, and are more highly regulated than most other consumer goods, so new vehicle technologies are slow to penetrate markets. It will probably take decades for most vehicles to be autonomous, and some motorists may resist them.
Optimistically, high-level autonomous vehicles will be safe and reliable by 2025. A few more years will be required for testing and regulatory approval, so by 2030, autonomous vehicles may be commercially available and allowed to operate in many areas. Suppose they follow the pattern of previous vehicle technologies during the 2030s. In that case, they will be expensive and limited in performance, sometimes unable to reach the desired destination or requiring human intervention when they encounter unexpected situations.

Impacts:

As about 90% of crashes involve a human error, autonomous vehicles will reduce crash rates and costs by 90% (Kok, et al. 2017; McKinsey 2016), but this overlooks additional impacts these technologies can introduce:

  • Hardware and software failures. Complex electronic systems often fail due to false sensors, distorted signals and software errors. Self-driving vehicles will certainly have failures that contribute to crashes, although their frequency is difficult to predict (Dawn Project 2022).
  • Malicious hacking. Self-driving technologies can be manipulated for amusement or crime.
  • Increased risk-taking. When travellers feel safer, they tend to take additional risks, called offsetting behaviour or risk compensation. For example, autonomous vehicle passengers may reduce seatbelt use, and other road users may be less cautious (Millard-Ball 2016), described as “over-trusting” technology (Ackerman 2017).
  • Additional risks to non-auto travellers. Autonomous vehicles may have difficulty detecting and accommodating pedestrians, bicyclists, and motorcycles (PBIC 2017).
  • Reduced investment in conventional safety strategies. The prospect of autonomous vehicles may reduce future efforts to improve driver safety (Lawson 2018).
  • Higher vehicle repair costs due to additional equipment. Additional sensors and control systems, and increased quality control, are likely to significantly increase collision repair costs.

The biggest impact on jobs that AVs will have is on drivers themselves. The whole point of autonomous vehicles is to have no driver controlling the vehicle. Companies and local businesses will hire qualified staff in the IT field to help with maintenance and troubleshooting any problems that AVs have. This would see a significant drop in the number of workers in the logistics and transport fields that companies and local business hire. We would also see an increase in electrical mechanics who would have to operate and complete maintenance checks on the AV itself.
